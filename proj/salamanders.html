Introduction

The Pacific giant salamander, Dicamptodon tenebrosus, are large salamanders native to the Pacific Northwest of North America. These salamanders are found around bodies of water such as creeks, ponds, and brooks in forests. Deforestation due to logging is a common threat to the species, so it is important to assess the effects of this disturbance (Johnston et al. 2002). Salamander data was collected at Mack Creek and provided by the Environmental Data Initiative (EDI). The only two measurements used in this report are the counts and weights for the Pacific giant salamander. This study presents annual salamander counts at two different forest sections of Mack Creek (clear cut and old growth).

Figure 1. Pacific giant salamander. Credit: Oregon Wild.

Data & Methods

Pacific giant salamanders were captured and weighed at Mack Creek in the H. J. Andrews Experimental Forest and data from 1987 – present were provided by EDI Data Portal (Gregory 2016). 150 m reaches are divided into 3 contiguous 50 m reaches, which are blocked off with nets. Each 50 m sub-reach is then sampled with two-pass electrofishing. For more specific geographic sampling coordinates, see metadata EDI Data Portal. The abundance and weight were compared at 3 channel classifications for 2 types of forest section by chi-squared test, two sample t-test, Levene’s test, one-way ANOVA and Tukey’s test (α = 0.05 throughout). All analyses and figures were prepared using R software version 3.6.1.

Figure 2. Map of Mack Creek area of study. Credit: EDI Data Portal.

Results

Salamander count in clear cut (CC) and old growth (OG) sections of Mack Creek from 1993 to 2017. Trends are fairly similar in both sections, with salamander abundance increasing sharply in 2002, decreasing rapidly around the year 2012, and then increasing again beginning in 2015. For all but two years from 1993 to 2014 salamander count in the old growth section was greater than in the clear cut section, with the two exceptions being 1994 and 2012, where salamander count in the clear cut section slightly exceeded salamander observations in the old growth section. Salamander count in the clear cut section was greater than in the old growth section for all three years from 2015 to 2017.
